# The Great Gatsby

F. Scott Fitzgerald

---

- Classic Literature
- Modernist Fiction

- Tragedy


---

# Themes

- The American Dream
- Social Class and Stratification
- Obsession and Memory
- The Illusion of Progress
- Morality and Decadence


---

# Summary (1/4)

Set against the backdrop of the Roaring Twenties, The Great Gatsby is a haunting exploration of the American Dream through the eyes of Nick Carraway. Nick, a young man from the Midwest, moves to West Egg, Long Island, and becomes ensnared in the orbit of his enigmatic neighbor, Jay Gatsby. Gatsby is a man of immense, mysterious wealth who throws extravagant parties in hopes of recapturing the heart of Daisy Buchanan, a woman from his past who is now married to the brutal and blue-blooded Tom Buchanan.

---

# Summary (2/4)

The narrative meticulously deconstructs the facade of the Jazz Age, revealing the hollowness beneath the glitter. Gatsby’s pursuit of Daisy is not merely a romantic endeavor but a desperate attempt to rewrite history and achieve a status that his humble origins deny him. Despite his grand efforts, he remains an outsider to the "old money" elite of East Egg, who possess a callous indifference toward those they deem beneath them. This social stratification serves as a central engine for the novel's tragic momentum.

---

# Summary (3/4)

Symbolism permeates the prose, from the recurring "green light" representing an unattainable future to the "Valley of Ashes," a wasteland signifying the moral and social decay resulting from uninhibited capitalism. The novel culminates in a series of violent events that shatter Gatsby’s illusions, underscoring the impossibility of reclaiming a lost past. Fitzgerald’s lyrical style captures the fleeting nature of time and the inevitable disappointment inherent in a dream built on material excess.

---

# Summary (4/4)

Ultimately, the story serves as a profound critique of the American ethos. It suggests that while individuals may strive to move forward, they are often "borne back ceaselessly into the past." The tragic demise of Gatsby, paired with the survival of the Buchanans, illustrates a world where wealth provides a shield against consequence, leaving the dreamer to perish alone in the wake of their own ambition.

---

# Key Points

- The sharp contrast between West Egg's 'new money' and East Egg's 'old money' dynamics.
- The symbolism of the Green Light as a beacon of both hope and the futility of reaching for the past.
- The Valley of Ashes representing the forgotten moral and physical wasteland of industrial society.
- Nick Carraway's complex role as an unreliable narrator and an objective moral observer.
- The fatal realization that Gatsby cannot recreate the past despite his vast wealth and efforts.
